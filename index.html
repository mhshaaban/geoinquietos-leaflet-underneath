What do I mean by "come alive"? In a normal tiled map in Leaflet, the background map is static. You can add vector data on top of a tiled map, and you can interact with it by clicking etc, but the background map is more or less a dead image. Here's a good example: lots of stores, restaurants etc, but I can't know anything about them except for sometimes their names. Still, that image is generated from one of the most impressive geographic databases we have, the OpenStreetMap database. What is shown in those images is just one side of all the information that is stored in the database.

Prior Art

So, I must confess I didn't come up with this idea entirely by myself. There's some prior art, let's look at out favourite competitor Google Maps As you can see, Google Maps actually shows points of interest on the map more or less as links, you can hover and you can click them. Clicking brings up a lot of information similar to what I showed you before.
Tiles are awesome, because they divide the problem of global map coverage into millions and millions of tiny images. What's not so awesome is that they are, ehrm, images. Raster images. They contain colour information, and the only "meaning" in them is the interpretation from our brains, by putting together the pixels. There's nothing to extract for further exploration here. So, we need a way to add information, on global scale, like tiles, but with some other information that just colour.

UTFGrid


I feel obliged just to mention UTFGrid, which is a technology to add interactivity to tiled maps. Its more or less raster tiles, where the "pixels" are not colour values, but indexes into an array of features. Since mouse clicks and finger taps are imprecise, UTFGrid tiles can be lower resolution than raster tiles, saving bandwidth. UTFGrid seems to have gone out of style, and even though a lot of tools support it, it's hard to find good UTFGrid sources for OpenStreetMap data. Potentially, UTFGrid could be a solution, but it would involve setting up your own UTFGrid tiles, which make it just too much work (for me). Look it up though, it's a simple and fascinating technology!

I should also mention OpenStreetMap's Overpass API, which is a fantastic way to query the OpenStreetMap database. It's versatile, but perhaps not aimed for quick client interaction. Something about sending an arbitrary query in a specialised query language to a server just doesn't sit right with me, it doesn't feel entirely 2016.

Vector Tiles

Something that does feel 2016 is Vector Tiles. So yeah, let's try vector tiles! Compared to UTFGrid, you can get easy access (Mapbox), good tooling if you want to roll your own. So, vector tiles in themselves can contain just about anything, but looking at Mapbox's "streets" vector tiles, they contain lots of POIs. Interestingly, they usually contain much more information than is usually shown in the end result. Normally this extra information is used to give the cartographer freedom to choose the amount of detail to show on the map, but it also suits us, since we want to be able to access more information than is shown. Inside, a tile is divided into layers, with different types of features inside each layer. For example, Mapbox streets has layer called poi_label, that sounds interesting.

Leaflet Underneath

So, lets examine. This is the same map as in my first example, but when I click it, I load the vector tile for that location, and examine the poi_label layer for nearby features. Good, but there's an apparent limitation: we can still only see type of feature, and its name. To save bandwidth, vector tiles leave out most of OSM's tags.
